# AI Discipline for Executives

**AI is not the job. AI is the leverage layer.**

The biggest risk AI poses to an executive is not that it will replace you. It is that it will quietly turn you into a junior engineer. The tools are so capable and so satisfying to use that a leader can spend a full week building, tuning, and refactoring — and produce nothing a shareholder, client, or team would recognize as progress. Discipline is what separates leverage from consumption.

This guide is about staying on the leverage side of that line.

## Build less, leverage more

Every hour you spend building a tool is an hour you are not doing the thing only you can do: setting direction, closing deals, resolving conflict, making the call. AI lowers the cost of building so dramatically that it feels irresponsible not to build. Resist that feeling. The question is never "can I build this?" It is "should the most expensive person in the room be building this?"

Leverage means getting a result without owning the labor. You leverage when you write a clear spec and hand it off. You consume when you open the editor yourself and start typing. Both can use AI. Only one scales you.

**Rule of thumb:** if a task would be a reasonable assignment for a capable contractor, it is not your job to do it — even if AI makes it fast.

## Protect executive focus

Your attention is the studio's scarcest asset. AI is an infinite source of plausible next steps, and each one arrives feeling urgent and tractable. Left unmanaged, this produces a day full of motion and empty of decisions.

Protect focus by deciding, in advance, what you are for. Most executives are for a small number of things: revenue, direction, key relationships, and the health of the team. Anything AI proposes that does not serve one of those is a candidate for deletion or delegation, not for your calendar.

Batch your AI work. Do not let it interrupt you all day. A leader who reaches for a model reflexively every few minutes has replaced thinking with prompting.

## Spec before build

The single highest-leverage habit is writing the spec before anything gets built — yours or the AI's. A spec forces you to decide what "done" looks like, what is in scope, and what success is worth. It converts a vague urge into a delegable unit of work.

Use **`/spec-first`** for this. A good spec answers: What outcome do we want? Who is it for? What does done look like? What is explicitly out of scope? What would make this not worth doing? Ten minutes of specification routinely saves days of building the wrong thing well.

The spec is also what makes delegation possible. You cannot hand off a feeling. You can hand off a spec.

## Delegate implementation

Once the spec exists, get it out of your hands. Implementation is where executive time goes to die — the fiddly, absorbing, endlessly-almost-finished work that AI is happy to help you do forever.

Use **`/handoff-to-cowork`** to move specced work to a collaborator or an agent with a clean brief. The handoff should carry the spec, the constraints, the definition of done, and the deadline. Then step back. Your job is to review the result against the spec, not to co-pilot the build.

If you find yourself unable to delegate a task, that is usually a sign the spec is not finished, not that the task is uniquely yours.

## Measure ROI

AI makes it cheap to start things and expensive to finish the wrong ones. Before committing real time, run **`/roi-check`**. Ask what this returns and what it costs — not just in money, but in your attention and the team's. A tool that saves ten minutes a month and takes two days to build is a loss you will feel every quarter you maintain it.

Kill projects that do not clear the bar. The willingness to stop is a competitive advantage; most people lack it because the sunk cost feels like a reason to continue.

## Avoid side quests

A side quest is work that feels productive but does not move an outcome: rebuilding a tool that already exists, polishing a dashboard nobody reads, refactoring code that works. AI is a side-quest machine because it removes the friction that used to make these detours obviously not worth it.

The defense is to name the outcome first and check every task against it. If you cannot say which real result a piece of work advances, you are on a side quest. (See `avoiding-ai-side-quests.md` for warning signs and a recovery protocol.)

## Maintain session continuity

Executive work is interrupted work. You will start something, get pulled into a meeting, and return hours or days later. Without continuity, you re-do the thinking every time — and AI sessions, which lose their context, make this worse.

Close every meaningful working session with **`/wrapup`**: capture what was decided, what is open, and what happens next. This turns a scattered day into a resumable thread. Over a week, feed those wrapups into `/project-memory` so the studio's context compounds instead of evaporating.

## The executive's short list

- Ask "should I be building this?" before "can I build this?"
- Write the spec first with `/spec-first`; a feeling can't be delegated, a spec can.
- Hand off implementation with `/handoff-to-cowork`; review against the spec, don't co-pilot the build.
- Run `/roi-check` before committing time; kill what doesn't clear the bar.
- Name the outcome before every task to stay off side quests.
- Close sessions with `/wrapup` so your context survives the interruptions.

Used this way, AI does what it should for a leader: it removes the reasons you used to be busy, so you can spend your time being decisive.
