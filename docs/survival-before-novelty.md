# Survival Before Novelty

**AI is not the job. AI is the leverage layer.**

When a team is under pressure, AI is dangerous in a specific way: it makes the interesting work as easy to start as the important work. A struggling studio can spend its last runway building a clever internal tool while the invoices go unsent. The tools do not know the difference between survival and novelty. You have to supply that judgment.

This guide gives you a fixed priority order. When you are deciding what AI work to do next — and especially when resources are tight — you walk down this list and do the highest item that applies. You do not skip ahead because something lower is more fun.

## The priority order

1. **Cash-flow visibility**
2. **Revenue generation**
3. **Cost reduction**
4. **Client delivery**
5. **Margin clarity**
6. **Risk reduction**
7. **Workflow speed**
8. **Internal convenience**
9. **Novelty**

Higher numbers are not "bad." They are just later. A studio that is healthy and well-funded can afford to spend time near the bottom. A studio under pressure cannot, and the mistake almost everyone makes is spending scarce time and attention on items 7–9 while items 1–3 go unattended.

## What each level means

**1. Cash-flow visibility.** Do you know what money is coming in, going out, and when? You cannot manage what you cannot see. AI's first job under pressure is to make the cash picture legible — summarizing receivables and payables, flagging what is overdue, projecting the next several weeks. This is not optional. Everything below it is a guess until this is solid.

**2. Revenue generation.** Work that brings money in: proposals, outreach, pricing, closing, upsells, shipping the thing customers pay for. If AI is going to save you anywhere, save you here first. Coding instead of selling is the classic fatal substitution — it feels like work and produces no revenue.

**3. Cost reduction.** Money you stop spending is as real as money you bring in, and often faster to capture. Use AI to find redundant subscriptions, over-provisioned services, manual work that shouldn't need a person, and vendors worth renegotiating.

**4. Client delivery.** Meeting the commitments you have already been paid for, or will be. Late delivery destroys the revenue relationships in level 2. AI helps here with drafting, review, QA, and keeping delivery on schedule — but never at the cost of the confidentiality rules in `ai-chain-of-custody.md`.

**5. Margin clarity.** Knowing which work, clients, and product lines actually make money after real costs. Many studios are busy and unprofitable at the same time and don't find out until it's too late. AI can model project margins so you stop selling more of what loses money.

**6. Risk reduction.** Reducing the chance of an event that could sink you: a contract gap, a compliance miss, a single point of failure, a security hole, an IP leak. Lower than delivery because a risk is a maybe; a missed payroll is a certainty. But do not let it fall off the list.

**7. Workflow speed.** Making the work the team already does go faster. Genuinely valuable — but notice it sits below everything about money and clients. Faster is only worth it if you're moving in the right direction.

**8. Internal convenience.** Nicer dashboards, tidier docs, smoother handoffs, a bot that formats standup notes. Pleasant. Rarely urgent. Easy to mistake for progress.

**9. Novelty.** "Let's see what this new model can do." Exploration, experiments, cool demos. There is a place for this — learning compounds — but it is the *last* place, not the first, and never on the critical path when survival is in question.

## How to apply it

**Filter every proposed piece of AI work with `/survival-filter`.** Before you start, ask which level it serves. If a task lives at level 8 and you have unresolved problems at level 2, the task waits. `/survival-filter` exists to make this a quick, honest reflex instead of a debate. When someone (including you) is excited about a build, the filter asks the uncomfortable question: *which level is this, and is anything above it unhandled?*

**Ground the top of the list with `/forecast-check`.** Levels 1, 2, and 5 depend on numbers you actually believe. `/forecast-check` pressure-tests your cash and revenue projections — the assumptions, the timing, the downside case — so that "cash-flow visibility" means a forecast you'd bet on, not a spreadsheet that looks tidy. Run it whenever a decision hinges on money you expect but haven't received.

**Re-rank when the situation changes.** The order is fixed, but which level *needs attention* changes weekly. When cash is secured for the quarter, you legitimately earn the right to spend more time lower down. When a big client wobbles, delivery and revenue snap back to the top. Reassess in your weekly rhythm (see `executive-ai-workflow.md`).

## The one-sentence test

Before any AI project, say out loud: *"If this succeeds perfectly, which of the nine levels does it move, and is anything above it currently unhandled?"* If something above it is unhandled, you have your answer — that comes first.

Survival buys you the runway to reach novelty. Novelty pursued before survival just shortens the runway.
